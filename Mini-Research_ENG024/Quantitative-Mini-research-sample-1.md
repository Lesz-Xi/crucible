## Abstract
This  study  examined  the  feasibility  of  transitioning  from  plastic  to  compostable  food
packaging in the Mapúa Malayan Colleges Mindanao (MMCM) canteen, focusing on cost
implications,   operational   and   environmental   benefits,   and   the   overall   cost-benefit
relationship.  Using  a  quantitative  descriptive-survey  design,  data  were  gathered  from  15
canteen  staff  members  directly  involved  in  food  preparation,  packaging,  and  waste
management.  Results  showed  that  while  procurement,  storage,  and  waste  management
presented high-cost implications (overall mean = 3.06), the operational and environmental
benefits  were  also  rated  high  (overall  mean  =  2.98).  Respondents  recognized  that
compostable packaging improved practicality,  encouraged proper  waste segregation, and
enhanced the canteen’s eco-friendly image. The cost-benefit analysis further revealed that
the  perceived  advantages—such  as  sustainability,  long-term  savings,  and  institutional
reputation—outweigh  the  financial  challenges  (overall  mean  =  3.07).  These  findings
indicate  that  shifting  to  compostable  packaging  is  both  feasible  and  beneficial,  though
initial   expenses   and   supply   accessibility   remain   significant   concerns.   The   study
underscores   the   importance   of   balancing   environmental   goals   with   financial   and
operational realities, suggesting that strategic planning and institutional support are key to
ensuring a successful transition toward sustainable packaging.
Keywords: Compostable packaging,  sustainability,  cost-benefit  analysis,  environmental
management, canteen operations

## 1. Introduction
The  growing  environmental  concern  over  plastic  pollution  has  placed  significant
pressure on institutions to explore more sustainable packaging  alternatives.  In particular,
university canteens face increasing demand to reduce their reliance on single-use plastics,
which  contribute  heavily  to  waste  generation  and  greenhouse  gas  emissions  (Solsona,
2024). Within the context of Mapúa Malayan Colleges Mindanao, the potential transition
from  conventional  plastic  food  packaging  to  compostable  food  packaging  raises  critical
questions  about  cost  efficiency  and  long-term  sustainability.  Shifting  to  compostable
packaging   is   not   a   straightforward   solution, as   it   involves   balancing   financial
considerations,  infrastructure  readiness,  and  consumer  acceptance.  While  studies  have
suggested that compostable options can generate long-term cost savings through reduced
waste disposal (Kaur & Acid, 2020), institutions may still hesitate due to higher material
costs. In the case of the Mapúa MCM canteen, such factors make it necessary to examine
whether  the  long-term  benefits  can  outweigh  the  immediate  financial  and  operational
constraints.   Equally   important   are   consumer   perceptions   and   behaviors   regarding
compostable  packaging.  Research  indicates  that  while  sustainable  materials  such  as
biodegradable  alternatives  promote  environmental  responsibility  (Giordano  &  Mariani,
2024;  Daramola  et  al.,  2025),  the  actual  success  of  such  initiatives  depends  on  student
awareness and participation. This presents a challenge in aligning institutional efforts with
consumer expectations, as gaps between intention and practice may hinder the effectiveness
of the transition (Solsona, 2024). This study focuses on conducting a cost-benefit analysis
of  shifting  from  plastic  to  compostable  packaging  in  the  Mapúa  MCM  canteen,  with
particular  attention  to  the  financial,  environmental,  and  behavioral  dimensions  of  this

## 4



change. As student-researchers, we view this research problem as both timely and essential,
especially  in  highlighting  how  academic  institutions  can  lead  in  adopting  sustainable
practices while addressing the realities of cost and implementation barriers.
International studies reveal that shifting from plastic to compostable packaging in
university canteens provides both environmental and economic advantages. Research at the
Middlebury  Institute  of  International  Studies  found  that  compostable  food  service  ware
(CSFW)  can  be  beneficial for  the  institutions,  with  positive  Net  Present  Values  (NPVs),
making it a cost-effective long-term option (Kaur & Acid, 2020). However, evidence from
Canada  suggests  that  reusable  packaging,  when  used  multiple  times,  may  be  more  cost-
efficient and environmentally beneficial compared to single-use compostable, showing that
frequency of use is an important factor in sustainability assessments (Snyder & Park, 2024).
Furthermore, studies highlight that bioplastics and renewable resources support economic
practices  and  the  United  Nations’  Sustainable  Development  Goals,  though  challenges
remain  in  terms  of  affordability,  usability,  and  infrastructure  requirements  (Ikechukwu,
2024; Daramola et al., 2025). In the Philippines, the move toward compostable packaging
also   presents   promising   outcomes   but   requires   careful   evaluation   of   costs   and
implementation.  Yao  et  al.  (2024)  found  that  biodegradable  materials  can  decompose
within a month, significantly reducing plastic waste and pollution. Similarly, innovations
like StrawPlast,  which  combine  straw  fibers  with  bioplastics,  provide  opportunities  for
promoting  a  circular  economy  and  minimizing  environmental  damage  (Giordano  &
Mariani, 2024). Still, studies emphasize that biodegradable packaging materials are often
more expensive than plastics due to production costs and the need for updated technologies,
though  long-term  savings  may  arise  from  reduced  waste  management  expenses  and

## 5



alignment  with  sustainability  policies  (Yao  et  al.,  2024;  Ikechukwu,  2024).  At  the  local
level, studies in Zamboanga City show that adopting plastic alternatives has reduced single-
use  plastic  consumption,  but  challenges  persist  in  recyclability,  biodegradability,  and
consumer acceptance (Mohammad, 2024). Stakeholder feedback indicates that while there
is support for environmentally friendly packaging, practical concerns regarding durability,
usability, and cost remain significant barriers. These findings highlight that for institutions
such  as  university  canteens,  including  the  Mapúa  MCM  canteen,  the  transition  to
compostable packaging requires balancing environmental goals with financial realities and
consumer behavior. As student-researchers, we recognize these constraints but believe that
analyzing both the long-term benefits and short-term challenges is critical in determining
whether such a transition is feasible and sustainable within the campus context.
The transition from plastic to compostable packaging in the context of Mapua MCM
can  be  effectively  guided  with  two  theoretical  lenses;  the Cost-Benefit  Analysis  Theory
(CBA)  and  the  Theory  of  Planned  Behavior  (TPB).    The  Cost-Benefit  Analysis  Theory
provides a systematic comparison on the costs of compostable packaging with its potential
benefits,  such  as  reduced  waste  disposal  expenses,  environmental  gains,  and  enhanced
institutional  reputation.  (Mishan  &  Quah,  2020).  In  the  context  of  the  Mapúa  MCM
canteen,  this  framework  enables  an  evaluation  of  whether  the  transition  can  generate
positive net value in the long term, despite challenges such as higher material costs. At the
same time, the Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB), formulated by Ajzen (1991, as cited in
Sarmah & Singh, 2024) stated that the TPB “posits that an individual's intention to engage
in a particular behavior is determined by their  attitudes, subjective norms, and perceived
behavioral control” (p. 03). The TPB explains that attitudes toward compostable packaging,

## 6



perceived social expectations, and the ease of adopting such changes influence students’
and consumers’ willingness to participate in and support the transition. For example, even
if  compostable  packaging  is  economically  justifiable,  resistance  from  consumers  due  to
perceived  inconvenience  or  indifference  toward  environmental  issues  may  hinder  its
success.  Together,  these  theories  provide  a  holistic  framework  that  integrates  economic
evaluation with behavioral insights. The combination ensures that the study does not only
address the financial feasibility of adopting compostable packaging but also considers the
social dynamics that determine whether the transition will be sustainable and effective in
practice in the Mapua MCM community.
Sustainable packaging has received increasing attention in recent  years; however,
several gaps remain in the existing research, specifically within the context of Philippine
educational  institutions. Many  studies  are  based  on  international  models, which  may  not
accurately  reflect  the  economic  conditions,  infrastructure  limitations,  and  behavioral
dynamics present in local university settings such as Mapúa MCM. For example, there is
limited  investigation  into  whether  campuses  have  the  necessary  systems  to  support
compostable    packaging,    including    waste    segregation    and    composting    facilities.
Additionally, while behavioral theories, such as the Theory of Planned Behavior, have been
widely applied, few studies have examined how Filipino students and faculty perceive and
respond  to  sustainable  packaging  initiatives.  The  role  of  canteen  vendors  is  sometimes
overlooked,  despite  their  involvement  in  implementation  and  their  concerns  about  cost,
durability, and supply. Reusable packaging is also often discussed as a viable alternative,
yet   studies   evaluating   its   practicality   alongside   compostable   options   in   campus

## 7



environments  are  scarce.  There  is  also a  need  to  examine  how  institutional  policies  and
local regulations either support or hinder these transitions.
This  study  aims  to  determine  the  feasibility  of  transitioning  from  plastic  to
compostable packaging by assessing the long-term viability for vendors and evaluating the
attitudes, perceived social pressures, and perceived behavioral control of the students and
professors  in  adopting  the  sustainable  practice  in  the  Mapúa  MCM  canteen.  This  study
seeks to understand how the transition can be effectively implemented within the campus,
considering  not  just  the  social  acceptance  but  also  the  economic  implications  for  the
canteen vendors. The goal of this study is to provide a better understanding that guarantees
the  shift  is  financially  viable  and  fully  supported  by  the  whole  campus  community  to
contribute in regulating the use of single-use products.

## 2. Methods
2.1 Research Design and Rationale
This   study   will   employ   a   quantitative   descriptive-survey   research   design.
According to Apuke (2021), a descriptive survey is a systematic method of gathering data
from  a  defined  group  of  respondents  using  questionnaires  or  structured  interviews  to
quantify  opinions,  behaviours,  or  perceptions  regarding  a  specific  issue.  This  design  is
appropriate  for  this  study  as  it  aims  to  obtain  measurable  insights  into  the  attitudes,
perceived challenges, and cost-related concerns of canteen staff toward shifting from plastic
to compostable packaging.
The use of a quantitative approach is justified because the study requires numerical
data  that  can  be  statistically  analysed  to  determine  patterns  and  trends in  responses
(Creswell & Creswell, 2018). By focusing on measurable indicators such as cost efficiency,
operational feasibility, and perceived environmental benefits, the study ensures objectivity
in  data  collection  and  analysis.  Furthermore,  this  design  is  consistent  with  the  goal  of
conducting  a  cost-benefit  analysis,  which  requires  structured,  quantifiable  data  (Kenton,
## 2021).
The  descriptive-survey  design  would  align  with  the  theoretical  foundation  of  the
study, the Theory of Planned Behaviour (TPB) by Ajzen (1991). The TPB emphasizes how
attitudes, subjective norms, and perceived behavioural control influence decision-making
and  behaviour.  Applying  this  framework  allows  the  study  to  capture  how  canteen  staff

## 9



perceive  the  practicality  and  challenges  of transitioning  to  compostable  packaging,
particularly in terms of financial implications and daily operations.
Conducting  the  study  at  Mapúa  Malayan  Colleges  Mindanao  (Mapúa  MCM),
provides contextual relevance because the canteen serves as a key site for waste generation
on campus. Understanding the perspectives of canteen staff, who are directly involved in
food preparation, packaging, and service, ensures that the data collected reflects first-hand
knowledge  of  the  costs,  workload  adjustments,  and  feasibility  issues  associated  with
replacing plastic with compostable packaging.
## 2.2 Research Respondents
The respondents of the study will consist of 15 canteen staff members employed at
Mapúa  Malayan  Colleges  Mindanao  in  Davao  City,  during  the  conduct  of  the  research.
This group was purposively chosen because they are directly involved in food preparation,
packaging,  and  service,  and  thus  have  the  most  relevant  knowledge  regarding  the
operational and cost implications of shifting from plastic to compostable packaging. Their
daily responsibilities give them direct exposure to issues such as cost efficiency, handling
procedures, storage requirements, and waste management, making their insights crucial for
assessing the practicality of the transition.
Because  the  total  population  of  canteen  staff  is  small,  the  study  will  employ  a
complete enumeration method. This refers to the collection of data from every member of
the  population  instead  of  relying  on  a  sample  (Lavrakas,  2008).  This  would  eliminate
sampling error and ensures that the findings reflect the full range of perspectives within the

## 10



canteen staff. It is suitable when the population is manageable in size, as in this case (Singh
## & Masuku, 2014).
All canteen staff members available at the time of data collection will be included
regardless of their roles (e.g., kitchen workers, servers, cashiers). Since each role engages
with  food  packaging  in  different  ways,  including  the  entire  group  allows  for  a  more
comprehensive understanding of the operational challenges and cost-benefit considerations
involved.
## 2.3 Data Collection
The  process  of  gathering  data  for  this  study  was  carried  out  in  a  straightforward
manner.  The  researchers  first  sought  verbal  consent  from  the  canteen  management  of
Mapúa Malayan Colleges Mindanao (MMCM) to allow the survey to be conducted among
their staff. After receiving approval, a researcher-prepared online survey questionnaire was
personally distributed to 15 staff members during their break times to avoid disrupting their
work. Before  answering  the survey, the respondents were informed about the purpose of
the  study,  which  was  to  examine  the  cost  implications,  operational  feasibility,  and
environmental benefits of shifting to compostable packaging. They were also assured that
their answers would remain anonymous and confidential. The questionnaire made use of a
four-point likert   scale   and   was   organized   into   sections   covering   cost,   operational
challenges,   and   environmental   perceptions.   The   information   collected   from   the
respondents’ answers became the primary basis for the study’s analysis.

## 2.4 Data Analysis Plan
The  questionnaires  were  reviewed  to  ensure  accuracy,  and  each  response  was
encoded and organized into tables for computation. Since the survey used a four-point likert
scale, the results were analyzed using frequency counts, percentages, means, and standard
deviations.  These  statistical  tools  helped  measure  the  views  of  respondents  on  cost
implication,  operational  and  environmental  benefits of  compostable  packaging.  The  data
were then grouped into three main sections, which included cost implications, operational
and  environmental  benefits,  and  cost-benefit  relationship.  Within  these  sections,  the
responses  were  examined  to  identify  patterns  and  trends.  These  findings  guided  the
evaluation  of  how  feasible  compostable  packaging  would  be  for  the  canteen  operations.
The statistical results were linked to the study objectives to provide clear insights into the
advantages,  limitations,  and  effects  of  the  shift.  The  analysis  prioritized  accuracy,

## REVIEW OF RELATED LITERATURE
## Cost Implications
The  transition  from  traditional  plastic  packaging  to  compostable  alternatives
requires not only an environmental lens but also careful financial consideration. A study by
Kaur and Acid (2020) at the Middlebury Institute of International Studies provides a strong
methodological foundation through its Cost-Benefit Analysis (CBA) and Net Present Value
(NPV) assessment of compostable food service ware (CSFW). Their findings showed that
compostable options, while initially more expensive, proved financially sustainable in the
long  run  and  environmentally  advantageous  compared  to  both  plastics  and  reusable
serviceware.  Importantly,  the  study  challenges  the  assumption  that  reusable  options are
automatically   superior,   since   these   also   carry   hidden   costs,   such   as   washing   and
maintenance.
From a local perspective, the “Study on Plastics Use and Waste Management in the
Food  Service  Industry”  in  Dasmariñas  City,  Cavite,  prepared  by  Step  Forward and
commissioned  by  PEMSEA  (2022),  highlights  systemic  barriers  to  reducing  plastic  use.
Despite   the   presence   of   a   local   ordinance   banning   plastics,   many   food   service
establishments (FSEs)—especially those handling take-away orders—continued to rely on
plastics.  Barriers  included  high  costs  of  alternatives  and  weak  waste  management
infrastructure.  These  findings  emphasize  that  a  successful  transition  to  compostables
requires not only initial procurement and storage adjustments but also financial feasibility
in waste management practices.

## Operational & Environmental Benefits
While  cost  is  a  central  issue,  the  practicality  and  environmental  benefits  of
compostable  packaging  are  equally  significant.  Cuerda  et  al.  (2023),  in  their  study  at
Talisay  City National  High  School,  found  that  sustainable  initiatives  such  as  green
marketing and eco-friendly packaging influenced awareness among students and faculty.
However, they also observed an intention–behavior gap, where positive  attitudes did not
consistently  translate  into  practice.  Grounded  in  the  Theory  of  Planned  Behavior  (TPB),
this research highlights how operational barriers like convenience and handling can limit
the effectiveness of compostable packaging adoption, even when attitudes are favorable.
Environmental  advantages  have  also  been  widely  documented.  Jones  and  Patel
(2019) demonstrated that single-use plastics, particularly polypropylene (PP), significantly
contribute  to  environmental  degradation  through  energy-intensive  production  and  waste
emissions.   Their   exploration   of   alternatives,   such   as   reusable   PP   containers   and
compostable   bagasse   clamshells,   revealed   improvements   in   waste   reduction   and
sustainability outcomes. Similarly, studies employing Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) tools,
such  as  SimaPro  and  TRACI,  provide  evidence  that  compostable  packaging  contributes
positively  to  reducing  plastic  waste,  supporting  waste  segregation,  and  improving  long-
term  campus  sustainability  (Jones  &  Patel,  2019;  NRC  &  Institute  of  Medicine,  1999  &
## 2000)
At  the institutional  level,  the  Step  Forward  &  PEMSEA  (2022)  report  further
emphasized that robust waste segregation systems are critical to maximizing the benefits
of  compostable  packaging.  Food  establishments  inside  malls,  for  instance,  showed  more
effective waste handling due to stricter enforcement, while independent operators lagged.

operational  efficiency  and  environmental  outcomes  only  when  supported  by  proper
infrastructure and institutional commitment.
Overall Cost-Benefit
The  reviewed  literature  collectively  suggests  that  the  adoption  of  compostable
packaging  entails  higher  initial  costs  but  leads  to  long-term  sustainability  benefits  when
combined  with  institutional  support  and proper  infrastructure.  While  financial  feasibility
(Kaur  &  Acid,  2020)  and  systemic  barriers  (PEMSEA,  2022)  highlight  the  challenges,
operational insights (Cuerda et al., 2023) and environmental assessments (Jones & Patel,
2019;  NRC  &  Institute  of  Medicine,  1999  &  2000)  affirm  that  compostable  packaging

evaluating  not  only  immediate  costs  but  also  the  broader  cost–benefit  relationship  in
ensuring a successful shift from plastics to compostables.
