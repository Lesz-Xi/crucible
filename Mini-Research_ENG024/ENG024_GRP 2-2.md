## 1.1 Introduction

The rise of cashless payments has significantly transformed the
way  students  handle  daily  expenses,  with  e-wallets  increasingly  used  for
food, goods, and other necessities (Benigno et al., 2024; Hu & Hou, 2024).
While these platforms provide convenience and efficiency, the researchers
recognize that this ease of use creates challenges in maintaining responsible
budgeting,  often  reducing  students’  awareness  of  their  spending  and
promoting impulsive financial behavior (Ambon et al., 2025). Specifically,
students  use  e-wallets  frequently  while  managing  academic  and  personal
responsibilities, which makes it challenging to track spending and maintain
responsible budgeting. This situation emphasizes the main problem of the
study, which is how students’ frequent use of digital payment platforms
affects their ability to track expenses and maintain effective budgeting.
Globally,  digital  payment  systems  like  e-wallets  have  become
widespread, leading to significant changes in consumer financial behavior.
Studies from Indonesia, for example, have shown that using digital payment
systems like e-wallets and mobile banking has changed how students spend
and  manage  their  money  by  making  transactions  easier  and  faster.  While
these  tools  can  help  students  control  their  finances  when  they  understand
how  to  use  them,  they  can  also  lead  to  impulsive  spending,  especially
among those with less financial awareness (Fikri & Susilowati, 2025).
In the Philippines, especially in Mabalacat City, the use of platforms
like GCash and Maya has become part of student life (Gamboa et al., 2025).

The accessibility of these tools allows students to quickly pay for necessities,
but it also introduces new challenges  in tracking expenses and sticking  to
the  budget.  Many  students  face  difficulties  in  monitoring  how  much  they
spend  daily,  which  leads  to differing  budgeting  behaviors  (Rifani  et  al.,
2024). Similarly, in Davao City, students encounter financial challenges, as
the convenience of e-wallet  transactions sometimes  results in inconsistent
budgeting (Dawa & Castro, 2024). These trends highlight the growing need
to  study  how  frequently  students  use  e-wallets  and  how  this  behavior
interacts with their budgeting practices, particularly among college students
who  are  learning  to  balance  academic  duties  with  personal  financial
management.
This investigation  is informed by  the  Financial Behavioral  Theory
developed  by  Barberis  and  Thaler  (2003).  According  to  this  theory,
individuals are not always logical or cautious in making financial decisions.
Rather, spending behaviors are determined through habit and emotion, and
also  by  the  manner  through  which  money  is  framed.  The  theory,  in  this
context, will help explain why students may frequently use an e-wallet and
how  it  influences  their  behavior  of  budgeting.  Since  the  e-wallets  make
payments faster and easier, students may spend more without even noticing.
At  the  same  time,  some  students  might  use  the  e-wallet  features,  like
tracking  tools,  in  managing  their  budget  better.  This  theory  is  relevant  to
this  study  because  it  shows  how  financial  behavior  is  formed  based  on
convenience, self-control, awareness, and personal decisions. Based on the

theory, this study will develop an understanding of how ENG 024 students
make  their  choices  regarding  the  use  of  e-wallets  and  the  frequency  of
monitoring their spending.
Existing research prioritizes e-wallet adoption patterns and general
spending habits over the specific frequency of use and its direct impact on
day-to-day  budgeting  behaviors  (Belmonte  et  al.,  2024;  Gamboa  et  al.,
2025). While studies examine broader consumer trends or financial literacy
interventions (Fikri & Susilowati, 2025; Rifani et al., 2024), fewer address
how  frequent  e-wallet  transactions  reshape  the   expense-tracking  and
planning  practices  of  first-year  college  students  who  are  newly  managing
their own finances independently. In the Philippine context, cultural factors,
such  as  reliance  on  family  financial  support  transitioning  to  personal
budgeting, and how digital payments disrupt these emerging habits remain
understudied  (Dawa  &  Castro,  2024).  This  study  fills  these  gaps  by
investigating   how   e-wallet   usage   frequency   specifically   influences
budgeting behaviors among MMCM ENG024 first-year students.
The  purpose  of  this  study  is  to  examine  how  the  frequency  of  e-
wallet use among MMCM ENG024 college students influences their basic
budgeting  practices,  including  planning,  tracking,  and  adjusting  daily
expenses.  By  focusing  on  MMCM  students  who  navigate  both  academic
demands and routine financial decisions, the research will describe patterns
of  digital  spending  across  common  expense  categories  such  as  food,

transportation,  school-related  needs,  and  personal  purchases.  Quantitative
survey  data  will  be  used  to  measure  e-wallet  usage  frequency  and  self-
reported  budgeting  behaviors,  while  descriptive  analysis  will  highlight
differences  across  spending  patterns  and  budgeting  strategies  within  the
group. The findings aim to offer MMCM and similar institutions practical
insights  for  developing  targeted  financial  literacy  initiatives,  supporting
students in managing cashless transactions responsibly, and guiding future
research on student budgeting behavior in an increasingly digital financial
environment.

## 1.2 Research Objectives
- To   determine   the   frequency   of   e-wallet   use   among   MMCM
ENG024 college students in managing daily expenses.
- To identify the budgeting practices that MMCM ENG024 students apply
when managing their expenses.
- To examine the types of expenses for which MMCM ENG024 students
typically use their e-wallets.

## 2 Methods
## 2.1 Research Design

This  study  employs  a  quantitative  descriptive  research  design  to
examine  the  frequency  of  e-wallet  use  and  the  budgeting  behaviors  of
MMCM ENG024 college students. A descriptive design is appropriate as it
focuses on systematically describing characteristics, behaviors, and patterns
within a specific population using numerical data by according to Nwabuko
(2024).  In  this  study,  the  design  allows  for  the  collection  of  measurable
information  regarding  how  often  students  use  e-wallets,  the  types  of
expenses  they  pay  for  using  these  platforms,  and  the  budgeting  practices
they apply in managing their finances.

The  use  of  a  quantitative  approach  is  justified  because  the  study
seeks  to  quantify  students’  e-wallet  usage  patterns  and  describe  their
budgeting  behaviors  through  structured  data  that  can  be  statistically
summarized  (Bayot  et  al.,  2023).  By  utilizing  a  survey  questionnaire,  the
research  gathers  numerical  data  to  identify  trends  and  common  practices
related to digital payment usage and financial management. This approach
supports  objectivity  in  data  collection  and  analysis,  ensuring  that  the
findings reflect observed patterns.

## 2.2 Research Respondents
The  respondents  of  this  study  consist  of  30  first-year  college
students  enrolled  in  ENG024  at  Mapúa  Malayan  Colleges  Mindanao  in

Davao City. This group was chosen because they are directly relevant to the
study's  objectives,  which  examine  the  frequency  of  e-wallet  use  and  its
influence  on  budgeting  behaviors  among  students  newly  transitioning  to
independent   financial   management.   As first-year   learners   balancing
academic   demands   with   personal   expenses,   they   possess   firsthand
experience   with   digital   payments   for   daily   needs   like   food   and
transportation, making their insights essential for understanding real-world
patterns of convenience versus spending awareness.
The  study  will  utilize  a  purposive  sampling  method  to  select  the  30
participants.  This  technique  involves  the  researcher  selecting  participants
based  on  their  specific  characteristics  and  knowledge  relevant  to  the
research  question  (Teddlie  &  Yu,  2007).  Purposive  sampling  is  highly
appropriate for this study because it focuses the data collection exclusively
on  participants  who  meet  critical,  pre-determined  inclusion  criteria:  (1)
current  enrollment  in  an  ENG024  section  at  MMCM,  (2)  being  an  active
user  of  any  e-wallet  platform  (e.g.,  GCash,  PayMaya,  etc.),  and  (3)
willingness   to   participate   voluntarily   and   provide   informed   consent.
Selecting respondents based on these criteria ensures that every participant
directly  contributes to  achieving  the  study's  core  objectives,  which  is  the
defining strength of the purposive method.

## 2.3 Research Locale
The  research  study  will  be  conducted  at  Mapúa Malayan  Colleges

Mindanao (MMCM). The selection of MMCM is based on the fact that the
respondents,  ENG024  students,  are  currently  enrolled  in  the  institution,
making  it  an  appropriate  and  accessible  setting  for  the  study.  MMCM  is
recognized as a technology-oriented higher education institution (Balba et
al.,  2023)  and  is  an  affiliate  of  Mapúa  University,  which  supports  the
relevance of the locale for a study focused on digital financial practices. The
school  setting  also  allows  for  easier  data  collection  through  a  controlled
environment  during  survey  administration.  Furthermore,  conducting  the
study  at  MMCM  provides  the  opportunity  to  gather  first-hand,  real-life
information  from  students  regarding  how  they  use  and  budget  their  e-
wallets in a technology-driven academic environment.

## 2.4 Research Instruments
The  primary  instrument  used  in  this  study  is  a  structured  online
survey  questionnaire  with  closed-ended  items  designed  to  measure  the
frequency of e-wallet use and the budgeting behavior of MMCM ENG024
college  students.  The  questionnaire  utilizes  a  Likert scale  to  quantify
respondents’ frequency of behaviors and levels of agreement. The research
instrument was adapted from the validated questionnaire developed by Pal
et al. (2020) in their study titled “Is the Convenience Worth the Risk? An
Investigation of Mobile Payment Usage.” Approximately 10 to 15% of the
original survey items were modified to suit the context of the present study.
The adaptations involved rewording selected statements and incorporating

examples  relevant  to  college  students  and  commonly  used  local  e-wallet
platforms.  The  questionnaire  is  designed  to  address  all  objectives  by
including  items  on  (1)  frequency  of  e-wallet  use,  (2)  budgeting  practices,
and  (3)  types  of  expenses  paid  through  e-wallets,  ensuring  that  each
objective   is   adequately   measured.   This   structured   format   supports
consistent  responses  and  reliable  quantitative  analysis.  The  survey  is
administered  through  Microsoft  Forms  to  facilitate  accessibility,  efficient
distribution, and systematic data collection.

## 2.5 Research Procedure
The    research    procedure    followed    a    systematic    process    consisting    of
conceptualization,  instrument  adaptation,  respondent  selection,  data  collection,  and  data
analysis.
Conceptualization and Literature Review. The study began with the identification
of  the  research  problem  and  objectives,  focusing  on  how  the  frequency  of  e-wallet  use
influences  budgeting  behaviors  among  first-year  college  students.  A  review  of  related
literature was conducted using both local and international academic sources to establish
the research gap and support the study’s variables. Existing studies primarily focused on
e-wallet  adoption  and  general  spending  behavior,  highlighting  the  need  to  examine  how
frequent digital transactions affect students’ budgeting practices. The findings from the
literature  review  guided  the  development  and  alignment  of  the  research  instrument  with
the study objectives.
Instrument  Adaptation.  The  survey  questionnaire  used  in  this  study  was  adapted

from the validated instrument developed by Pal et al., (2020) in their study titled “Is the
Convenience Worth the Risk? An Investigation of Mobile Payment Usage.” The original
instrument focuses on mobile payment usage behavior and frequency. Approximately 10-
15% of the original items were modified to suit the context of MMCM ENG024 first-year
college students. The adaptation process involved rewording selected statements for clarity,
simplifying terminology, and incorporating examples relevant to students’ daily expenses
and  commonly  used  local  e-wallet  platforms.  Additional  items  related  to  budgeting
practices,  such  as  planning,  tracking,  and  adjusting  daily  expenses,  were  included  to
address the research objectives while maintaining the structure and validity of the original
scale. A 5-point Likert scale was retained to ensure consistency in measuring responses.
Respondent   Selection.   The   study   employed   purposive   sampling   to   select
participants who met the specific  criteria of the research. A total of 30 first-year college
students  enrolled  in  ENG024  at  Mapúa  Malayan  Colleges  Mindanao  (MMCM)  were
chosen  as  respondents.  These  participants  were  selected  because  they  represent  students
who are beginning to independently manage their daily finances, making them appropriate
for examining the relationship between e-wallet usage frequency and budgeting behavior.
Data Collection  and Ethical Considerations. Data were collected using Microsoft
Forms, which allowed for efficient distribution and automated recording of responses. Prior
to answering the survey, respondents were presented with an informed consent statement
explaining the purpose of the study, the voluntary nature of participation, and the assurance
of  anonymity  and  confidentiality.  No  personally  identifiable  information  was  collected,
and all responses were used solely for academic research purposes.
Data Analysis. After the completion of data collection, the responses were reviewed

to ensure completeness and accuracy. Descriptive statistical methods were used to analyze
the data. These methods were applied to describe the frequency of e-wallet use, budgeting
behaviors, and types of expenses paid through e-wallets. The results were then interpreted
in relation to the study’s objectives.

2.6 Trustworthiness of the Study
The  trustworthiness  of  the  study  is  ensured  through  the  use  of  a
structured  and  standardized  survey  questionnaire  adapted  from  related
studies.  The  instrument  is  reviewed  to  ensure  clarity  and  relevance  to  the
objectives of  the research. Data is  collected using the same procedure for
all  respondents  to  maintain  consistency.  Additionally,  the  responses  are
collected  anonymously  to  guarantee  accurate  responses  and  minimize
response bias, assuring that the data appropriately reflects the respondents'
e-wallet usage, budgeting practices, and types of expenses.

## 2.7 Ethical Consideration
The study observes ethical standards to ensure the rights and well-
being  of  all  participants.  Respondents  are  made  aware  of  the  study's
objectives and are entitled to withdraw participation at any moment without
facing  any  repercussions.  Every  response  is  collected  anonymously  and
handled with the utmost confidentiality, preserving the security of sensitive
information.  The  data  gathered  will  be  used  solely  for  academic  research
purposes, and care is taken to present findings with integrity and accuracy.
