Globalization, Education, and Indigenous Rights

1. Introduction

We often hear that globalization makes the world smaller, flatter, and more connected. In our textbooks, it is described as a "borderless" phenomenon that opens limitless doors for economic opportunity and cultural exchange. But for us students, and especially for those of us belonging to Indigenous Cultural Communities (ICCs), globalization is not just a doorway; it is often a double-edged sword. On one hand, learning international standards—specifically English language proficiency—is sold to us as the golden ticket to the future. It is the key to finding jobs abroad, working in Business Process Outsourcing (BPO) industries, or competing in the global market. On the other hand, this pursuit of global competitiveness often demands a silent but heavy price: the gradual setting aside of our own local culture, languages, and epistemologies to "fit in" with the dominant global order.

In this paper, I will critically analyze this tension. I will examine how global educational standards, particularly in English Language Teaching (ELT), function as tools of pressure that affect our local traditions. By looking through the lens of globalization theories like Cultural Homogenization and Glocalization, and by evaluating the legal protections offered by the Indigenous Peoples’ Rights Act (IPRA), I hope to answer a fundamental question: Can we become globally competitive students without ceasing to be Indigenous people? This analysis argues that while current educational trends push for uniformity, our laws and our local resilience offer a path toward "Critical Glocalization"—a way to learn the world’s language without forgetting our own voice.

2. The Problem: Becoming All the Same (Cultural Homogenization)

The assigned reading on "Cultural Contexts in English Language Teaching" serves as a springboard for understanding the pressures we face. It highlights how educational standards are often imported directly from Western contexts without modification. In the study of globalization, this phenomenon is best explained by the theory of Cultural Homogenization (often referred to as Cultural Convergence).

Basically, homogenization implies that globalization is making all cultures look the same, modeled after the dominant Western cultures (typically American or European). In the educational context, this is what sociologist George Ritzer might call the "McDonaldization" of Education. Just as a Big Mac tastes the same in Davao as it does in New York, our education system attempts to produce students who think, speak, and write in a standardized, predictable "global" way.

The Mechanism of Exclusion

Why is this a problem? It becomes an issue when "Standard English" is taught not just as a skill, but as a superior way of thinking.

  • The "English Only" Policy: Many of us grew up in schools with "English Only Zones" where speaking our native dialect or mother tongue was punishable by fines or shame. This sends a psychological message early on: Global is good/smart; Local is bad/backward.
  • Standardized Testing: Global benchmarks like IELTS (International English Language Testing System) or TOEFL measure our ability to communicate, but they often use context that is foreign to us. They prioritize Western logic, directness, and argumentation styles. When we struggle with these not because we lack intelligence, but because our cultural communication style is different (perhaps more circular or respectful), we are labeled as "deficient."
  • Erasure of Knowledge: As a result, Indigenous Knowledge Systems and Practices (IKSP) are pushed to the sidelines. We might learn about Shakespeare’s sonnets in depth, but leave school knowing almost nothing about the oral epics of our own elders. We are trained to be workers for the global west, rather than guardians of our local heritage.

3. The Alternative: Mixing Global and Local (Glocalization)

However, globalization does not purely have to be a destructive force for local culture. The study suggests an alternative path: Glocalization (or Cultural Hybridization). This concept argues that global forces always interact with local realities, creating something new.

In education, "localization" or glocalization means adapting global standards to fit the local context. It rejects the idea that we must strip away our identity to learn.

  • Anchoring the Lesson: Instead of teaching complex English grammar rules using sentences about winter snow or Western urban life, teachers can use sentences that describe our local farming practices, our festivals, or our values of bayanihan.
  • Validation of Content: Imagine an English literature class where we analyze the translated text of a Manobo epic with the same rigor we apply to Beowulf. This is glocalization. It achieves the same global goal (analytical thinking and English proficiency) but uses local substance to get there. It shows us that our stories are "worthy" of academic study.

4. The Legal Battlefield: The Role of IPRA

We are fortunate that in the Philippines, we have a legal weapon to fight strictly homogenized education: the Indigenous Peoples’ Rights Act (RA 8371) or IPRA. Passed in 1997, this law is a landmark legislation intended to correct historical injustices.

Education as a Right, Not a Privilege

Several sections of IPRA directly address our education:

  • Section 28 (Integrated System of Education): This is crucial. It mandates the State to provide education that is "relevant to the needs" of Indigenous children. This legal provision attacks the "one-size-fits-all" curriculum. If a curriculum forces us to forget our roots, it is by definition not relevant to our needs as Indigenous people.
  • Section 30 (Educational Systems): This is even more radical. It explicitly recognizes the right of ICCs to "establish and control their educational systems" and provides that education should be in our "own language" and consistent with our cultural methods of teaching.
  • Section 31 (Recognition of Cultural Diversity): This obligates the educational system to eliminate prejudice.

The Gap Between Law and Reality

However, as I analyze the current situation, I must critique the implementation. IPRA often functions as a "Paper Shield." It gives us rights on paper, but the "Economic Sword" of globalization pierces through it.

Agencies like CHED (Commission on Higher Education) and DepEd (Department of Education) are under immense pressure to improve the country's rigorous PISA scores and employability ratings. This leads to curricula that prioritize global alignment over IPRA compliance. IPRA is often treated as a suggestion—fulfilled by wearing traditional attire once a year—rather than a structural mandate to change how and what we teach every day.

5. A Personal Perspective: The View from SPAMAST-JAS

(Note: This narrative reflects a representative experience of a student in our context.)

As a student here at SPAMAST-JAS Extension, and as a member of the Indigenous Cultural Minority, this analysis is not just academic to me; it is my lived reality. I walk this tightrope every single day.

I remember a specific instance in one of my communication classes that really highlights this tension. We were asked to deliver persuasive speeches. I wanted to talk about a traditional practice of conflict resolution in my tribe—a system that focuses on restoration rather than punishment. However, during the drafting phase, I found myself constantly translating concepts that didn't have direct English equivalents. I struggled to make it sound "professional" or "academic" because the English format demanded a linear, aggressive "Problem-Solution" structure, whereas our way of discussing conflict is more circular and community-based.

In the end, out of fear of getting a low grade or being misunderstood, I switched my topic to "The Benefits of Social Media Marketing." It was generic, it was "global," and it was easier to fit into the rubric. I got a high grade, but I felt a hollow sense of defeat. I had successfully demonstrated my "global competitiveness," but I had done so by silencing my own Indigenous voice.

This happens constantly. We are often praised when we sound least like ourselves. We are taught that to be "educated" is to be "Westernized." When I go home to my community, I sometimes feel a disconnect, as if the education I am fighting so hard to get is slowly building a wall between me and my elders. This is the "Effect Estimation" of unmitigated globalization: it creates a generation of skilled workers who are strangers in their own ancestral lands.

6. Conclusion and Recommendations

In conclusion, globalization is a powerful current that pulls us toward uniformity. Through the mechanism of cultural homogenization, global educational standards threaten to erode the unique identities of Indigenous students. However, this is not inevitable. Through the strategy of Critical Glocalization, and by leveraging the unused power of the IPRA Law, we can push back.

To truly address this, I propose the following:

  1. For Curriculum Developers: Stop treating Indigenous Knowledge as an "add-on" subject or an elective. Integrate it into core subjects. Use local history to teach political science; use local weaving patterns to teach mathematics; use local oral traditions to teach literature and grammar.
  2. For the Government (CHED/NCIP): There needs to be an audit of how Section 28 of IPRA is implemented in State Universities. We need assurance that our education is equipping us to defend our domains, not just preparing us to leave them.

We must redefine what it means to be "educated." It should not mean replacing our local soul with a global mind. Instead, it should mean expanding our local roots deep enough that we can grow tall without being uprooted by the winds of the global world. We can be JASenyos, we can be Indigenous, and we can be global—all at the same time.
